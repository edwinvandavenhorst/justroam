Maiden Trip: Bosnia & Slovenia

Our first overland trip ever — a last-minute pivot from Norway to the Balkans, wild horses, a scary narrow trail, and a dog who couldn't cross into Montenegro.

At a glance

  • Why we went: Norway felt too uncertain for a first-ever overland trip (weather, wild camping, none of us had done it before), so with days to go we pivoted south, first eyeing Albania, then let the route unfold as we drove.
  • Best moment: Tracking down a herd of wild horses on a remote plain in Bosnia and flying the drone over them — and two magical nights on a Slovenian hilltop overlooking the vineyards.
  • Hardest part: Reversing the truck out of a trail that got too narrow, with a slipping clutch and a scorched smell to prove it — plus discovering the fridge and solar panel drawer had taken damage from the rocky terrain.
  • Would we do it again: Yes — never felt rushed or crowded, and it made us fall in love with Una park.

The route vibe

Just a few days before departure we were still undecided where to go. Norway had been the original plan for our maiden trip, but the rugged camping and unpredictable weather gave us pause — none of us had real experience camping in the wild, and the basic questions (bathroom, showering, safety) were still unanswered. Then, without anyone quite remembering whose idea it was, Albania came up. One look at the map and we decided to just go for it.

On departure day the truck was packed and we were excited to go, with a loose plan to drive until around 4pm each day and find a spot for the night. First stop was [ABC], a simple but clean harbourside camping with good facilities at a great price. Next stop: Triglav, Slovenia — until a weather check a few hours in revealed 13°C days and 8°C nights there. Too cold for what we wanted, so we pivoted south on the spot, planning to swing back to Triglav on the way home once the weather improved.

We found a spot [ABC] on a hill overlooking the Slovenian vineyards, and it was so good we stayed two nights — wine, sunset over the vines, and Edwin discovering the joys of a long outdoor shower. This, we realised, is what overlanding actually is: no fixed plan, just going with the flow.

Planning the days ahead, we hit our first real snag: our dog Izzy couldn't enter Montenegro without the right paperwork (a tick treatment / tether test we simply didn't have), so rather than risk it we pivoted to spending more time in Bosnia instead of using it as a thru-route. Getting there meant a short stretch through Croatia first — where we discovered we weren't allowed to eat anywhere with the dog, so lunch became an improvised supermarket picnic in a small town. Then it was on to the border crossing out of the EU, eSIMs ready.

Finally in Bosnia, we stocked up and drove to our spot for the night: a camping right inside the park with minimal facilities but the clearest water we'd stayed by yet. Over dinner we found out we could go rafting, and booked it on the spot for the next day. Two brothers ([DEF]) picked us up for a multi-hour rafting tour with occasional stops to jump in the water — fun for the whole family, Izzy included.

We stuck around the area for a few more days — hikes, time by the water — then moved outside the park to a place called Status Quo. To this day we haven't had better facilities. The kids never wanted to leave: there was a free canoe and paddle board, and the spot was never busy, which made it a real sanctuary. We promised the kids we'd come back on the way home.

Next we went looking for a plain with wild horses. No guided tour, just us and the car. Finding the entrance took some trial and error, and once we were on the plain we drove deeper and deeper without seeing anything — Edwin was quietly working out the "what if we get stuck out here" scenario. Then we spotted a herd, drove off-trail onto rocky terrain to get close, and got pictures and drone footage of them. SOOOOO COOOL.

Following a trail we'd found on maps.me, things went fine until it narrowed more than we liked — shrubs, bushes, fresh scratches in the paint. With no way forward, the only option was reversing out on the same trail, which left the clutch slipping and the car smelling scorched. Not something to repeat. Back on the main road, a check in the back confirmed the damage: the fridge had come open and spilled, and its weight had squashed the drawer housing the solar panels — first item on the list to fix once home.

From there we headed to the highest point in Bosnia, a herders' town called [ADD: town name], reached via 40km of bumpy, sharp-stoned gravel — we kept passing regular passenger cars crawling along at 5km/h over every pothole. The town's single restaurant was the only place to stay, and it wasn't the best night's sleep: something that sounded exactly like howling wolves surrounded us. To this day we don't know if it was wolves or herding dogs. Earplugs recommended either way.

One more stop: a 4.5-hour hike to a lake in Montenegro, up another gravel mountain road overtaking crawling passenger cars. The hike itself was fantastic — gravel paths into a valley full of flowers, into forest, and eventually across the border to the lake, where the restaurant host checked our passports. We'd made it to Montenegro after all, dog and all. A peaceful lunch, a swim (Edwin never passed up water on this trip), then the hike back through forest and valley to the bare rock and gravel.

Looking for more to explore inland, a night on a busy road with some genuinely heavy thunder changed our minds, and we decided to close out almost two weeks in Bosnia with one more night back at our favourite spot, Status Quo. We planned two days of just chilling in the water — until a call from friends in Austria changed everything. They invited us on a dog-walking hike the next day, so we packed up for a spontaneous drive to Austria. Campsite prices there gave us sticker shock (80 euro a night versus 20 in Bosnia, excluding dog, electricity, and facilities), so we found proper accommodation — a real bed, shower, and kitchen — for about the same price. The next day we joined our friends' hike around the Weissee, which brought back memories of the hikes we used to do with our previous dog, Menno.

It's good to end a trip on a high, and that hike was exactly that. The next day it was back to the Netherlands and regular life — but not without amazing memories from our maiden trip, a genuine love for Una park, and the realisation that overland travel, done this way, never once felt rushed or crowded. It was a new and thoroughly enjoyable way to travel.

Highlights

Practical notes

Real notes from the trip — not exhaustive, but the things that actually mattered on the road.

  • Pet travel: Check entry requirements well ahead — we couldn't take our dog into Montenegro due to missing tick/tether paperwork, which reshaped our whole route.
  • Roads: Ranged from smooth asphalt to rocky mountain tracks and a 40km bumpy gravel trail up to a herders' town — some stretches genuinely needed 4WD and patience.
  • Weather: Highly variable by altitude — hot in the valleys, but we skipped Triglav on the way down because of 13°C days / 8°C nights.
  • Camping: Wild camping is very doable; official sites (like Status Quo in Una park) can beat anything you'd expect on facilities.
  • Connectivity: eSIMs worked well for data once outside the EU.
  • Dining with a dog: Don't assume restaurants will seat you with a pet — have a backup (supermarket) plan.

What we'd do differently next time

  • Scout unmarked 4x4 trails (like the one we found on maps.me) before committing — the one that got too narrow cost us paint, a clutch scare, and a squashed solar panel drawer.
  • Sort pet travel paperwork (tick treatment / tether requirements) before the trip, not en route.
  • Pack earplugs if staying overnight near [ADD: herders' town name] — the howling (wolves or herding dogs, we still don't know) made for a rough night.
